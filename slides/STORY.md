# The story

## The real problem

No contractor got into the trades to do paperwork. Your dad didn't. Your stepmom didn't. Nobody did.

But the invoice is the last thing standing between a finished job and getting paid. You can't skip it, and you can't afford to get it wrong. So at the end of a 10-hour day you sit down and try to remember what parts you used, what you charged last time, whether you remembered the trip charge.

Usually something gets missed.

## When mistakes add up

You forget a part, undercharge on labor, miss the trip charge. The vendor comes back with questions and now you're doing it twice. Next month you can't remember what you charged so the numbers are different again, the client notices, and you end up in a back-and-forth that exists entirely because you were tired when you wrote the invoice.

## Leaving money on the table

Inconsistent rates and forgotten line items add up fast. A contractor working 20 jobs a month who undercharges by $50 per invoice is leaving $12,000 a year on the floor -- and most don't realize it because they have no reference point when they're writing the invoice at 9pm. Foreman surfaces the history. It flags when something looks off. It makes sure you're charging what you're worth.

## What Foreman actually does

It doesn't replace the contractor's judgment. They still approve the invoice. It just means they're not starting from a blank page at 6pm after a long job. The tedious part -- pulling together parts, rates, descriptions, checking consistency against past invoices -- that's handled. The human makes the call.

## One sentence

Your dad didn't go into contracting to sit at a kitchen table at 9pm trying to remember if he charged for the refrigerant. Foreman handles that part.
